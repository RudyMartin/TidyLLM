1) What It Means to Patent Software / Mobile App Processes

A software patent protects the process your software performs, not the literal source code. Think of it as protecting a technical method or system implemented by the software, not the files on GitHub. 
The Rapacke Law Group
+1

For mobile apps or software, the valuable part is usually the novel process, logic flow, system interaction, or machine-executed improvement that the software carries out. 
The Rapacke Law Group

Mobile apps can also have multiple patents: utility patents for processes, and design patents for visual/UI elements. 
The Rapacke Law Group

Bottom line: You want to describe what your software does differently in technical terms, not just what the screens look like.

2) Basic Requirements for Patentability (Software)

A software-related invention must generally meet these criteria:

Novelty (new): It must not already exist in prior art (patents, publications, products). 
The Rapacke Law Group

Non-obviousness: It cannot be an obvious combination of existing ideas to someone skilled in the field. 
The Rapacke Law Group

Useful: It has some practical, real-world application. 
BitLaw

Machine-tied / implemented: The inventive process must be executed on a machine (i.e., a computer) — pure abstract ideas don’t get patents. 
The Rapacke Law Group

These are the same criteria the USPTO uses when examining utility patents. 
USPTO

3) What a Provisional Patent Application Is

A provisional application is not examined by the USPTO and will never become a patent on its own. 
Wikipedia

Its main purpose is to secure an early filing date (“priority date”). You then have one year to file a non-provisional (full) application. 
Wikipedia

After you file it, you can legally use the term “Patent Pending”. 
Wikipedia

Key point: The provisional must already teach your invention clearly. A weak or vague provisional can weaken your priority benefit later. 
Wikipedia

4) What to Focus on When Drafting Your Provisional

For software/mobile-app tech, a strong provisional draft usually includes:

Technical Narrative

Step-by-step description of how your solution works (process flows, modules, interactions).

Concrete examples of inputs/outputs, states, transitions — not pseudo-marketing.

Identify why the approach is better (performance, accuracy, user efficiency, resource use).

Claims or Claim Drafting Concepts

Even though Provisional applications in the U.S. do not require formal claims, describing your invention in claim-like terms (what it does) strengthens the disclosure.

Think in terms of method steps (e.g., process steps executed by the device), systems (modules interacting), and functional blocks.

Drawings & Diagrams

Include flowcharts for processes.

Include architecture diagrams showing components, data flow, state transitions.

Specificity

Define algorithms, data structures, protocols, APIs, rules that are novel.

Avoid vague language (“fast,” “best,” “improved”) without measurable criteria.

5) Why This Matters for a Provisional

The USPTO won’t examine the provisional directly, but the later utility (non-provisional) application will be judged on what’s disclosed now. 
Wikipedia

Anything not clearly described in the provisional might not get the benefit of the early filing date, exposing you to intervening prior art risks.

6) Patent Strategy & Next Steps

Prior Art Search

Before you finish your provisional, do or commission a search for similar patents/tech; this informs drafting and claim coverage. 
patentlawyer.io

Transition to Non-Provisional

Within 12 months you should prepare a detailed non-provisional with formal claims. Someone experienced with software patents can do this efficiently.

Responding to USPTO Examination

After filing the non-provisional, you’ll likely get Office Actions (rejections/objections) you must respond to. Understanding how claims are evaluated (novelty/non-obviousness) is essential.
